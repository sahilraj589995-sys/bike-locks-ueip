**1. Bike Lock Types**

*   **U-Locks (D-Locks):**

    *   *What it is:* A stiff metal U shape.
    *   *Good stuff:* Super tough to cut or pry open; small so you can clip it to your bike.
    *   *Not-so-good:* Not very big, and they're kinda heavy. You need something solid to lock to.
    *   *Best if:* You're in a city, parking for a bit.
*   **Chain Locks:**

    *   *What it is:* A thick metal chain with a lock.
    *   *Good stuff:* You can wrap it around stuff that's big or weirdly shaped.
    *   *Not-so-good:* Heavy. A determined thief with big bolt cutters could get through a thinner one.
    *   *Best if:* You're in a place where bikes get stolen a lot, locking to poles or fences.
*   **Cable Locks:**

    *   *What it is:* A flexible steel cable with a lock.
    *   *Good stuff:* Light and easy to carry.
    *   *Not-so-good:* Easy to cut with basic tools. Not very secure.
    *   *Best if:* You're in a safe spot, just popping in somewhere quick, or as a second lock.
*   **Folding Locks:**

    *   *What it is:* Metal plates linked together that fold up.
    *   *Good stuff:* Easy to carry, okay security, works in different situations.
    *   *Not-so-good:* heavier than cables, U-locks are stronger for the same price.
    *   *Best if:* You bike to work and want something portable that's not too flimsy.
*   **Wheel/Frame Locks:**

    *   *What it is:* Attached to your bike frame and stops the back wheel from turning.
    *   *Good stuff:* Always there, quick to use for a fast stop.
    *   *Not-so-good:* Only keeps the bike from being ridden; someone could still pick it up and carry it.
    *   *Best if:* You're just running into a place for a sec, area isn't known for theft.

**2. Lock Types**

*   **Keyed Locks:**

    *   *Good stuff:* Work well, tougher to pick than some combos.
    *   *Bad stuff:* Lose your key will cause problems.
*   **Combination Locks:**

    *   *Good stuff:* You don't need keys.
    *   *Bad stuff:* Skilled thieves can crack them. The numbers can get stuck.
*   **Smart/Bluetooth Locks:**

    *   *Good stuff:* Opens with your phone, can track your bike.
    *   *Bad stuff:* Needs batteries, cost more, might not work if it's super cold/hot.

**3. What Locks Are Made Of (and How Secure They Are)**

*   **Steel:** Strong, lasts a while. Thicker means harder to cut.
*   **Hardened Steel:** Even harder to cut or break.
*   **Alloy/Light metals:** Not as secure, more for being easy to carry.

    *   **How thick should it be?**
        *   Less than 8mm: Not very safe.
        *   8–12mm: Okay security.
        *   12mm+: Pretty secure (if it's a U-lock or chain).

**4. How to Stay Safe from Bike Thieves**

*   Lock your bike's frame and back wheel to something that can't be moved.
*   Don't use skinny posts – thieves can lift the bike over or just cut through the post.
*   Use two locks if you can to protect the front wheel.
*   Keep the lock off the ground, so it's harder to mess with.
*   Park where it's busy and has good lighting – thieves don't really like an audience.
*   Change where you lock your bike to avoid being predictable .

**5. Things to Avoid**

*   Don't use thin cables in areas where bikes are stolen often.
*   Don't just lock the front wheel.
*   Don't lock to things that can be moved (like small tree branches).
*   Don't leave your bike in dark, lonely spots.

**6. Security Levels (In General)**

*   **Low:** Thin cable locks, small combo locks.
*   **Medium:** Folding locks, average chains and U-locks.
*   **High:** Thick, hardened steel U-locks or chains, with double locking.

7. How Bike Thieves Steal Bikes

Understanding their tricks helps you lock up smarter.

A. Cutting Stuff

Bolt cutters: They’re the go-to for flimsy cables and cheap chains.

Angle grinders: Can slice through almost anything, but they're loud and take time.

Hacksaws: Good for thinner or soft steel.

B. Using Force

Crowbars/Prying: Used to crack open U-locks or bend weak chains.

How to stop it: Get thick, strong steel locks; keep locks off the ground.

C. Picking and Cracking Codes

Picking: Pro thieves can pick locks.

Code Cracking: They try to feel or hear the right combo.

D. Breaking and Twisting

Lock Snapping: Works on specific cylinder locks.

Wheel Grabs: Wheels and seats can get nicked if they aren’t locked up too.

8. Pro-Level Locking

A. “Double Locking”

Use a U-lock for the frame and back wheel.

Add a cable or folding lock for the front.

Think about a small lock for the seat and anything else easy to steal.

B. Where You Put the Lock

Lock the frame, a wheel, and something that can’t move.

Position the lock high up.

Leave as little space as possible inside the U-lock (so tools can't fit).

C. Switching It Up

Don't always lock in the same spot, so thieves don't know what to expect.

Don’t leave your bike in one place for too long.

D. Protecting Extras

Take off lights, bags, GPS – anything easy to remove.

Some locks let you secure the seat, wheels, and frame all together.

9. Keeping Locks Alive

Keep locks working smoothly with graphite or silicone spray.

Stop rust: Dry locks after rain; get weather-proof locks if your bike lives outside.

Check the locks: Make sure the key or combo still works smoothly.

Look at chains/cables: If they’re frayed or rusty, they’re getting weak.

10. Cool New Locks

A. Alarm Locks

If someone messes with it, it screams.

Keeps casual thieves away.

Usually paired with a U-lock or chain.

B. Smart Locks

Opens with your phone or fingerprint.

Has GPS tracking if it gets stolen.

Some send alerts if the lock moves.

C. Folding Locks

Mix of chain and plates – flexible but strong.

Lighter than chains but tougher than cables.

11. Making Thieves Think Twice

Be seen: Park where people are walking.

Scare them off: Complex locks make thieves think twice.

More is better: Two locks are better than one.
